Solo Practice Attorneys

Maximize your practice efficiency with AI-assisted client intake and case management.

Client Intake Questionnaire GeneratorCOSTAR
[Context] You are helping a solo practice attorney in [practice area: family law, personal injury, estate planning, etc.] create comprehensive client intake questionnaires.
[Objective] Generate a detailed intake questionnaire that captures all relevant information for a [case type] matter while being client-friendly and thorough.
[Style] Professional but accessible—legal precision without intimidating language.
[Tone] Welcoming, organized, thorough.
[Audience] Prospective clients seeking legal representation in [practice area].
[Response] Create an intake questionnaire including: (1) Personal Information - contact details, preferred communication method, emergency contact; (2) Case Background - description of legal issue, timeline of events, parties involved; (3) Prior Legal History - previous attorneys, related cases, court orders; (4) Documentation Checklist - relevant documents to bring, deadlines client is aware of; (5) Goals and Expectations - desired outcome, timeline expectations, budget considerations; (6) Conflict Check Information - opposing parties, related businesses or individuals.
[Case Details] Practice area: [Specify]. Case type: [Specific matter]. Jurisdiction: [State/local]. Special considerations: [Any unique factors].
[Constraints] HIPAA/confidentiality compliant language. Clear instructions. No legal advice in questions. Easy to complete in 15-20 minutes.
Why this works:
Thorough intake prevents downstream problems—missed deadlines, conflict issues, and incomplete case evaluation. COSTAR's audience-focused approach ensures questionnaires are comprehensive for attorneys while remaining accessible to clients without legal backgrounds. The constraint on avoiding legal advice in questions protects you from inadvertently creating an attorney-client relationship before engagement.

2026 Compliance: Under ABA Model Rule 1.18 (and state equivalents), prospective client information is protected even without formal engagement. Intake forms should include confidentiality disclosures and conflict check notices. If collecting sensitive information (medical records for PI, financial data for family law), ensure your intake process complies with applicable privacy regulations. Never input prospective client information into AI systems without appropriate confidentiality protections—this could waive privilege or violate duty of confidentiality.
Case Summary GeneratorCRISPE
[Capacity] You are a legal writing assistant helping an attorney create clear, comprehensive case summaries for internal use and client communication.
[Role] Legal documentation specialist with expertise in [practice area].
[Insight] I need to create a case summary for [case type] that captures the essential facts, legal issues, and strategic considerations. This will be used for [purpose: client updates, internal review, co-counsel briefing].
[Statement] Create a case summary including: (1) Case Caption - parties, court, case number, judge assigned; (2) Procedural History - key dates, filings, rulings to date; (3) Statement of Facts - chronological narrative, key disputed facts, undisputed facts; (4) Legal Issues - primary claims/defenses, applicable law, key precedents; (5) Current Status - pending motions, upcoming deadlines, discovery status; (6) Strategic Assessment - strengths, weaknesses, recommended next steps; (7) Client Communication Points - what client needs to know, decisions needed.
[Personality] Precise, analytical, action-oriented.
[Experiment] Include a one-paragraph executive summary suitable for quick review.
[Case Information] Provide: key facts, parties involved, legal claims, current status, upcoming deadlines.
[Constraints] Objective analysis. Flag uncertainties clearly. No speculation on outcomes. Maintain attorney-client privilege considerations.
Why this works:
Case summaries are living documents that keep matters on track. CRISPE's structured approach ensures all critical elements are captured while the "experiment" component (executive summary) provides quick-reference value for busy attorneys. The constraint on flagging uncertainties prevents the dangerous assumption that AI analysis is complete—you're prompted to verify and fill gaps.

2026 Compliance: Case summaries may be work product subject to privilege—be cautious about how and where they're stored. Under ABA Model Rule 1.1 (competence), attorneys must understand AI limitations; verify all case citations and legal analysis independently. Some federal courts now require disclosure of AI use in legal filings (standing orders in NDTX, other districts following). Never input case facts into AI without considering whether the information could be used to train models accessible to opposing counsel.

Legal Document Drafting AssistantCOSTAR
[Context] You are assisting a paralegal in drafting [document type: motion, discovery request, contract, letter] for a [practice area] matter in [jurisdiction].
[Objective] Create a well-structured draft that follows proper legal formatting and includes all necessary components for this document type.
[Style] Formal legal writing—precise, clear, properly cited.
[Tone] Professional, authoritative, appropriate for court/opposing counsel/client.
[Audience] [Court, opposing counsel, client, or other recipient].
[Response] Draft the document including: (1) Proper Caption/Heading - formatted per jurisdiction requirements; (2) Introduction/Purpose - clear statement of document purpose; (3) Factual Background - relevant facts with record citations where applicable; (4) Legal Argument/Substantive Content - organized by issue, supported by authority; (5) Conclusion/Request - specific relief or action requested; (6) Signature Block - proper format for jurisdiction.
[Document Details] Document type: [Specify]. Matter: [Brief description]. Key points to include: [List]. Relevant authority: [Cases, statutes]. Deadline: [If applicable].
[Constraints] Follow local rules for formatting. Include placeholder brackets for case-specific details. Flag areas requiring attorney review. Use jurisdiction-appropriate citations.
Why this works:
Document drafting is time-intensive but often follows predictable patterns. COSTAR's audience-aware structure ensures drafts are appropriate for their recipient (court, opposing counsel, or client), while the constraint on flagging attorney review areas maintains proper supervision. Placeholder brackets for case-specific details prevent the paralegal from inadvertently submitting incomplete work.

2026 Compliance: AI-assisted legal documents must still meet the supervising attorney's professional standards—attorneys sign and take responsibility for all filings. Multiple federal courts (including NDTX, EDPA, and others) now require certification that AI-assisted filings have been verified for accuracy. AI systems frequently "hallucinate" case citations; every citation must be verified in primary sources. Local court rules on formatting, page limits, and required attachments override any AI-generated formatting.
Research Summary MemoCRISPE
[Capacity] You are a legal research assistant helping a paralegal compile and synthesize research findings into a clear, actionable memo.
[Role] Legal research specialist supporting case preparation.
[Insight] I've researched [legal issue] and need to present findings to the supervising attorney. The research covers [scope: statutory law, case law, regulations, secondary sources].
[Statement] Create a research memo including: (1) Issue Presented - clear statement of legal question; (2) Brief Answer - concise answer with key takeaway; (3) Statement of Facts - relevant facts that frame the analysis; (4) Discussion - analysis organized by sub-issue, synthesis of authorities, application to facts; (5) Conclusion - summary of findings, recommendations for next steps; (6) Appendix - list of sources reviewed, key case summaries.
[Personality] Thorough, analytical, objective.
[Experiment] Include a "gaps in research" section identifying areas needing further investigation.
[Research Details] Legal issue: [Specify]. Jurisdiction: [State/Federal]. Key sources found: [List]. Deadline: [If applicable].
[Constraints] Cite all sources properly. Distinguish binding vs. persuasive authority. Note circuit splits or unsettled law. Objective—present both sides.
Why this works:
Research memos synthesize complex legal landscapes into actionable guidance. CRISPE's "gaps in research" experiment is particularly valuable—it prompts critical thinking about what's missing rather than false confidence in completeness. The constraint on distinguishing binding vs. persuasive authority prevents the common error of relying on out-of-jurisdiction cases without acknowledgment.

2026 Compliance: This is where AI "hallucinations" are most dangerous. AI systems frequently generate plausible-sounding but fictional case citations. Every case cited must be verified in Westlaw, Lexis, or official court databases—not just that it exists, but that the cited proposition is accurate. ABA Formal Opinion 512 (2024) clarified that relying on unverified AI research may violate Rule 1.1 (competence). The "Mata v. Avianca" sanctions should remind all legal professionals of the consequences of unverified AI citations.

Client communication impacts both satisfaction and malpractice risk. 2026 Compliance: Client communications may become evidence of the attorney-client relationship's scope, so accuracy matters. Under state bar ethics rules, non-attorney staff cannot provide legal advice in client communications. Email subject lines should not reveal confidential case information if delivered to shared/work accounts. For deadline reminders, ensure calculations comply with applicable rules of procedure and clearly note any caveats about deadline accuracy.

Mediators & Arbitrators

Prepare thoroughly for dispute resolution with structured case analysis and settlement frameworks.

Case Preparation AnalysisCOSTAR
[Context] You are helping a [mediator/arbitrator] prepare for a [type of dispute: commercial, family, employment, construction] proceeding by analyzing the case materials and identifying key issues.
[Objective] Create a comprehensive case analysis that identifies the core issues, party positions, and potential paths to resolution.
[Style] Neutral, analytical, comprehensive—no bias toward either party.
[Tone] Objective, thoughtful, solution-oriented.
[Audience] The neutral (for preparation purposes only).
[Response] Develop an analysis including: (1) Case Overview - parties, nature of dispute, procedural history; (2) Party Positions - each party's claims, defenses, stated interests; (3) Key Issues - legal issues, factual disputes, emotional/relationship factors; (4) Evidence Summary - key documents, testimony, expert opinions; (5) Range of Outcomes - best/worst case for each party, likely litigation outcome; (6) Barriers to Resolution - identify obstacles, party dynamics, emotional factors; (7) Potential Solutions - creative options, zone of possible agreement, process recommendations.
[Case Materials] Dispute type: [Specify]. Party positions: [Summarize]. Key facts: [List]. Amount in controversy: [If applicable]. Prior settlement efforts: [History].
[Constraints] Maintain strict neutrality. Identify both strengths and weaknesses for each side. No predetermined outcomes. Consider non-monetary interests. Flag ethical considerations.
Why this works:
Effective mediation requires deep preparation without prejudging outcomes. COSTAR's neutrality constraint is critical—mediators lose effectiveness (and face ethical issues) when they favor one side. The "zone of possible agreement" analysis helps mediators identify where creative solutions might emerge, while the barriers section anticipates emotional and relational obstacles that pure legal analysis misses.

2026 Compliance: Mediator confidentiality rules vary by state—analysis materials may be protected or discoverable depending on jurisdiction. Under ADR ethics codes, mediators must maintain impartiality; AI analysis should be equally rigorous for both parties. For arbitrators, ex parte communications restrictions apply—ensure analysis materials aren't shared asymmetrically. If case involves employment discrimination or other civil rights issues, be aware of EEOC mediation protocols and fair process requirements.

[Role] Settlement design consultant for complex disputes.
[Insight] I'm mediating a [dispute type] where the parties appear far apart. I need to develop settlement options that might bridge the gap and address underlying interests beyond stated positions.
[Statement] Create a settlement framework including: (1) Interest Analysis - underlying interests for each party beyond positions, shared interests, conflicting interests; (2) Value Creation Options - ways to expand the pie, non-monetary terms, creative solutions; (3) Structured Settlement Options - payment terms, contingencies, performance-based elements; (4) Risk Allocation - how to address uncertainty, insurance/guarantees, dispute resolution for implementation; (5) Relationship Terms - ongoing obligations, communication protocols, confidentiality; (6) Implementation Plan - timeline, milestones, monitoring mechanisms.
[Personality] Creative, practical, solution-focused.
[Experiment] Propose one "outside the box" solution that neither party has likely considered.
[Dispute Details] Nature of dispute: [Describe]. Party interests: [What each really wants]. Obstacles: [Why settlement is difficult]. Resources available: [What parties can offer]. Ongoing relationship: [Yes/No, nature].
[Constraints] Legally enforceable terms. Realistic implementation. Address enforcement concerns. Consider tax implications. Protect confidentiality where needed.
Why this works:
Impasse often occurs when parties focus on positions rather than interests. CRISPE's "outside the box" experiment pushes AI beyond conventional settlement structures, potentially surfacing creative options the mediator can introduce. The constraint on legal enforceability ensures creative ideas remain practical—a brilliant settlement that can't be enforced creates new problems.

2026 Compliance: Settlement agreements must comply with contract formation requirements in the applicable jurisdiction. For employment settlements involving discrimination claims, EEOC guidance and applicable state laws may restrict certain confidentiality provisions. Tax implications of structured settlements should be reviewed by qualified tax professionals. If settlement involves ongoing obligations, consider choice of law and dispute resolution clauses for enforcement.

Notaries

Support clients with clear explanations and proper procedure guidance.

Document Explanation GuideCOSTAR
[Context] You are helping a notary public prepare client-friendly explanations of common documents and the notarization process, without providing legal advice.
[Objective] Create a clear, accessible explanation of [document type: power of attorney, affidavit, deed, acknowledgment] that helps clients understand what they're signing.
[Style] Educational, clear, reassuring—avoiding legal jargon while maintaining accuracy.
[Tone] Helpful, patient, professional.
[Audience] General public with varying literacy levels and legal knowledge.
[Response] Create an explanation including: (1) Document Purpose - what this document does in plain language, common situations where it's used; (2) Key Components - main sections explained simply, what each part means; (3) Signer Requirements - who must sign, identification needed, mental capacity considerations; (4) Notarization Process - what the notary will do, why each step matters; (5) Important Considerations - what signers should know, when to consult an attorney; (6) Common Questions - FAQ for this document type; (7) Next Steps - what happens after notarization, where documents typically go.
[Document Details] Document type: [Specify]. State: [For jurisdiction-specific requirements]. Common use case: [Why people typically need this]. Special requirements: [If any].
[Constraints] No legal advice—explain, don't recommend. Use plain language (8th grade reading level). Include "consult an attorney if..." guidance. Accurate to state requirements. Emphasize signer's responsibility to understand.
Why this works:
Many people sign documents they don't understand, creating legal problems down the road. COSTAR's plain language constraint ensures explanations are accessible to all literacy levels, while the "consult an attorney" guidance creates clear boundaries between education and advice. The emphasis on signer responsibility protects notaries from claims that they exceeded their role.

2026 Compliance: Notaries are prohibited from practicing law (unauthorized practice statutes vary by state). Explaining what a document does differs from advising whether someone should sign it—AI-generated explanations should clearly stay on the "explain" side. For remote online notarization (RON), ensure explanations note any state-specific requirements. Some documents (powers of attorney, deeds) may require specific witness or notarization requirements that vary by state—always verify against current state law.

[Review Details] Industry: [Specify]. Regulatory area: [Specify]. Company size: [For context]. Known concerns: [If any]. Prior audits: [Results if available].
[Constraints] Current regulatory requirements. Prioritize by risk level. Practical, implementable recommendations. Clear accountability. Document retention guidance.
Why this works:
Compliance reviews often produce overwhelming reports that never get implemented. COSTAR's risk-prioritized approach ensures the most critical gaps get addressed first, while the implementation roadmap makes recommendations actionable. The audit-ready style creates documentation that serves multiple purposes—internal guidance, board reporting, and regulator response.

2026 Compliance: Compliance frameworks must reflect current regulations—2026 brings significant changes including Colorado AI Act requirements, updated HIPAA Security Rules, and evolving state privacy laws (CPRA, VCDPA, etc.). For AI use in compliance consulting, be aware that the Colorado AI Act may require disclosure when AI systems assist with "consequential decisions." Document the scope and limitations of any compliance review to manage client expectations and consultant liability. Recommendations should always note where qualified legal counsel review is advised.

[Experiment] Include a "black swan" scenario—low probability but catastrophic impact risk.

Why this works:
Risk assessments often miss critical issues by focusing only on obvious risks. CRISPE's "black swan" experiment forces consideration of low-probability, high-impact scenarios that traditional analysis overlooks. 2026 Compliance: Legal risk assessments may be subject to attorney-client privilege if prepared by or under the direction of counsel—structure engagement accordingly if privilege protection is desired. For M&A transactions, ensure due diligence covers emerging AI-related regulations that may affect the target company. Risk assessments involving AI systems should consider compliance with state AI laws (Colorado AI Act, California transparency requirements) and emerging federal guidance. Document the basis for risk probability and impact estimates to defend methodology if challenged.

Legal AI Ethics & Best Practices

Using AI Responsibly in Legal Practice
  • Confidentiality first. Never input client-identifying information or privileged communications into AI systems without proper safeguards and client consent.
  • Verify everything. AI can hallucinate case citations and legal principles. Always verify AI outputs against primary sources.
  • Maintain competence. Understand AI's capabilities and limitations as required by professional responsibility rules.
  • Supervise appropriately. AI is a tool, not a substitute for professional judgment. Lawyers remain responsible for all work product.
  • Disclose when required. Some jurisdictions and courts require disclosure of AI use. Know your obligations.
  • Protect work product. Be aware of how AI systems handle and store the information you input.

Your Expertise + AI Efficiency = Better Client Service

Legal AI is about working smarter, not replacing judgment. Use AI to handle routine tasks, accelerate research, and improve communication—so you can focus on the strategic thinking and client relationships that define excellent legal practice.
